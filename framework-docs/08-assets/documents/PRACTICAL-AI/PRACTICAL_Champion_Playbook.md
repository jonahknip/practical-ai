## HANDLING COMMON OBJECTIONS

### "AI will replace my job"

**Response:**
"I understand that fear - I had it too. But here's what I've learned: AI replaces tasks, not jobs. It does the repetitive stuff you hate, so you can focus on the work that requires your expertise and judgment. Think of it like Excel replacing calculators - it made accountants more valuable, not obsolete."

**Then show them:**
"Here's what AI does for me: [boring task]. Here's what I still do: [interesting, valuable work]. I actually feel more secure because I'm more productive and doing higher-value work."

---

### "I don't have time to learn this"

**Response:**
"That's exactly why we're doing this - to save you time. How about this: give me 15 minutes. I'll show you one thing AI can do that will save you 30 minutes this week. If it doesn't work, you're only out 15 minutes."

**Then deliver:**
Show them ONE quick win specific to their work. One task automated = believer created.

---

### "I tried it and it didn't work"

**Response:**
"That's actually really helpful feedback. Can you show me exactly what you tried? Usually it's just about how we phrase the request."

**Then diagnose:**
- Was the prompt too vague?
- Was the task too complex for current setup?
- Do they need a different tool?
- Is there a training gap?

**Then fix it:**
Show them the better way, or escalate if it's a real limitation.

---

### "This is too complicated"

**Response:**
"You're right that it CAN be complicated, but it doesn't have to be. You don't need to understand how it works - just like you don't need to know how email works to use it. Let me show you the 3 things you'll actually use 90% of the time."

**Then simplify:**
Give them a cheat sheet with exactly what to copy-paste for their top 3 use cases.

---

### "I don't trust AI - it makes mistakes"

**Response:**
"You're absolutely right - AI makes mistakes, just like spell-check sometimes gets it wrong. That's why we always review its work. Think of AI as a really fast intern: great for the first draft, but you're still the expert who checks quality."

**Then demonstrate:**
Show a before/after where you caught and fixed an AI mistake. This builds appropriate trust.

---

### "Our work is too specialized/complex for AI"

**Response:**
"I thought that too! But AI isn't trying to do your expert work - it's doing the administrative wrapper around it. You're still doing the specialized analysis; AI is just formatting the report, organizing the data, or drafting the email."

**Then prove it:**
Find ONE small administrative task in their workflow and automate it.

## YOUR SUPPORT TOOLKIT

### Quick Start Guide (Give to New Users)

**Step 1: Start with ONE task**
Don't try to AI everything at once. Pick one annoying, repetitive task.

**Best first tasks:**
- Writing emails/messages
- Summarizing long documents
- Creating first drafts of reports
- Data formatting
- Meeting notes

**Step 2: Use this simple prompt template**

```
I need help with: [describe task]

Context: [any background needed]

Format: [how you want output]

Example: [show what good looks like]
```

**Step 3: Review and refine**
AI's output is a starting point. You're the expert - edit it.

**Step 4: Save what works**
Build your own library of prompts that work for your tasks.

---

### Common Use Cases Cheat Sheet

**Email Drafting:**
```
"Write a professional email to [recipient] about [topic]. 
Tone should be [formal/friendly/urgent]. 
Keep it under 150 words."
```

**Document Summarization:**
```

[paste data]"
```

### Troubleshooting Guide

**Problem:** AI output is too generic/vague

**Solution:** Add more specific details and examples to your prompt

**Before:**
"Write a project update"

**After:**
"Write a project update for the Smith Street Bridge project. Include: current % complete (87%), upcoming milestone (final inspection Jan 30), budget status (on track), and one risk (weather delays possible). Tone: factual and brief for executive audience."

---

**Problem:** AI gets facts wrong

**Solution:** Always verify facts. Use AI for format/structure, you provide the data.

**Strategy:**
"Here are the facts: [paste verified data]
Format this into a report with sections: [list sections]"

---

**Problem:** Output is too long/short

**Solution:** Specify length in prompt

**Examples:**
- "Keep under 200 words"
- "3-5 bullet points maximum"
- "One page summary"
- "Expand this into 3 paragraphs"

---

**Problem:** Tone is wrong (too formal, too casual, etc.)

**Solution:** Specify exact tone in prompt

**Examples:**
- "Tone: conversational but professional"
- "Write as if explaining to a 10-year-old"
- "Formal tone for executive audience"
- "Friendly and enthusiastic for client"

---

**Problem:** "I don't know what to ask for"

**Solution:** Start with "Help me [verb] this [thing]"

**Examples:**
- "Help me summarize this report"
- "Help me format this data"
- "Help me write a response to this email"
- "Help me create a checklist from these notes"

### Setting Up Office Hours

**When:** [Pick consistent time, e.g., "Every Wednesday 2-3pm"]

**Where:** [Your desk / Conference room / Virtual]

**Format:** Drop-in, no appointment needed

**What to bring:** Laptops + specific task they want to automate

---

### Office Hours Agenda

**Minutes 0-5: Welcome**
- "What brought you here today?"
- "What task is eating up your time?"

**Minutes 5-20: Diagnose**
- Watch them do the task manually
- Ask: "How often do you do this?"
- Ask: "What's the most annoying part?"
- Identify the automatable pieces

**Minutes 20-45: Build Together**
- Don't do it FOR them, do it WITH them
- Let them drive (you guide)
- Create the prompt together
- Test and refine together
- Save the working version

**Minutes 45-55: Practice**
- Have them try it again without your help
- Answer questions
- Troubleshoot issues

**Minutes 55-60: Next Steps**
- "Use this 3 times this week"
- "Come back next week if issues"
- "Think of other tasks like this"
